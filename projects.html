University Projects

Using a Cypress PSoC 5LP Microcontroller, our team prototyped an automatic flow-control valve for perfusion pumps. Using a biological signal as input, such as heart rate or body temperature, the valve is able to regulate medication flow injected in the patient. The scope of our design was to offer a solution to urgent medication needs, that are at times difficult to provide since medical staff are spending less and less time with each patient.

The PSoC 5LP Microcontroller runs on a ARM Cortex-M3 core microchip, built on a 32-bit architecture, capable of state-of-the-art embedded digital signal processing. Our prototype wasn't tested on actual perfusion pumps, but the proof of concept showed a functional automatic flow-control process and a stable state-machine loop.

My Third-Year project was individual and open. During my 2018 internship in Haiti, I collected ideas of medical devices that were most needed, or that were difficult to repair in low-resource countries. My goal was to create a low-cost, low-maintenance effective device that could be used about anywhere, with very few material resources. Autoclaves are a major part of an hospital capacity to provide patient-care. They are often in bad shape, need specific replacement parts or simply misused. This leads to incomplete sterilizing processes and infected surgical instruments, which are then used in surgery and expose patients to major risks.

I've decided to build a solar-powered autoclave, using a Fresnel focal Lens to heat water and produce steam, that would then be carried upwards to sterilize medical instruments. A PSoC 5LP Microcontroller was again used to monitor temperature inside the chamber and calculate the sterilization cycle, to ensure the process was done thorougly. The prototype was a success, being able to maintain the required 125 Celsius temperature during 30 minutes without interruption. At the Electrical Engineering projects competition, this solar-autoclave won the 3rd prize of the jury.

All year-long, our team of 4 developed a prototype MRI antenna, in collaboration with the Montreal Institute of Cardiology, for micro-lesion brain imaging. Using resonant microelectronics circuits, our antenna was tuned at 299.464 MHz and was designed to improve image resolution by a factor 3. The medical scope of the project was to improve micro-lesion detections and facilitate scientific research linking early loss of cognition to micro-lesions in the brain. This antenna is designed to fit and be implanted on a live mouse skull during MRI imaging sessions.

Using condensers and coils to create very-high frequency magnetic resonance (7T MRI at 299.464 MHz), this antenna is completely non-magnetic and matched at an impedance of 50 Ohms to optimize image resolution and minimize artefacts. It was tested multiple times in in-vivo imaging sessions on one mice subject, and showed great promise, boasting a signal-to-noise ratio superior to commercial antennas.

Engineering Internships

My first internship took place in two haitian hospitals of Cap-Haitien, in the north of Haiti. With a team of four engineering students, we developed a medical equipment management software in Python, installed it in the two hospitals and provided a 2-month training to local biomedical technicians on management and repairs, using our homemade software. From preventive maintenance to replacement part orders, our training aimed at durability and high-impact improvements.

I learned a lot in this internship, understanding the key factors and challenges that shape humanitarian and international aid, as well as the political situation in Haiti. I also understood that generic, low-cost and effective medical equipments were lacking. Medical technology companies are developing and selling high-performance, high-cost instruments that are unfit to serve low-resource countries. I would like to design such equipments, not regulated by high-cost FDA rules, that would be easy to repair, effective and aimed to help countries that do not need multi-million medical machines, but simple and reliable instruments. It's my goal for the next 30 years!

As an embedded software developer at Rheolution, I took part in microcontroller programming on ST Microelectronics embedded boards (ARM Cortex-M7 core). This company develops mechanical testing instruments to measure viscoelasticity of various soft tissues. For cheese processing to medical biogels, the applications of their technology are various. I had the chance to work on Internet-of-Things features, as well as embedded digital signal processing solutions.

My final internship was at a worldwide giant of medical technology, called Zimmer Biomet. Based in Warsaw, Indiana, the company has R&D offices around the world. The Montreal division is focused on computer-assisted surgeries, mainly for Total Knee and Total Shoulder replacements (arthoplasty).

I worked as a System Designer on the recently released Signature ONE Shoulder system, that provides a surgery-planning software as well as intra-op cut and drill patient-specific guides. I had the chance to work in a highly-regulated Agile environment, with international standards such as ISO13485 and FDA 21 CFR regulations.
